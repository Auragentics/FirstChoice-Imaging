## **MRI**

##### **What is an MRI?**

**MRI stands for magnetic resonance imaging. It uses a powerful magnet and radio waves (no X-rays) to create detailed images of the inside of your body.**

##### **What is MRI best used for?**

**MRI is one of the best ways to clearly see the soft tissues of the body – like muscles, tendons, ligaments, nerves, and organs – that don’t show up well on X-rays. It helps doctors find the cause of pain, injury, or other symptoms without using any radiation.**

##### **What are some common reasons people get an MRI?**

**Spine and Nerves**

* **Back or neck pain**  
* **Herniated or bulging discs**  
* **Sciatica or pinched nerves**  
* **Spinal stenosis or nerve compression**

**Joints and Muscles**

* **Knee, shoulder, or hip pain**  
* **Torn ligaments or tendons (like ACL or rotator cuff)**  
* **Sports injuries**  
* **Arthritis or joint inflammation**

**Brain and Head**

* **Chronic headaches or migraines**  
* **Dizziness or balance problems**  
* **Memory or vision changes**  
* **Tumor, stroke, or multiple sclerosis evaluation**

**Abdomen and Pelvis**

* **Liver, kidney, or pancreas concerns**  
* **Uterus, ovaries, or prostate evaluation**  
* **Unexplained abdominal or pelvic pain**

**Other Areas**

* **Foot or ankle pain**  
* **Wrist, hand, or elbow injuries**  
* **Soft tissue masses or swelling**  
* **Vascular or blood vessel evaluation**

**MRI gives detailed images that help your provider make accurate, confident diagnoses – so you can get answers and treatment faster.**

##### **Do you take insurance?**

**Yes\! We accept most insurance plans. We also offer a simple self-pay option. To learn more about our payment options, [click here](https://firstchoice-imaging.com/payment-options/).**

##### **Is MRI safe? What if I have metal or implants?**

**Yes, MRIs are safe for most people. Because the magnet is strong, you’ll complete a safety screen.** 

**If you have a medical implant or device such as a pacemaker, spinal stimulator, or any other implanted hardware, please call us before your appointment. Our technologists must review the safety information for your device and confirm that it is compatible with MRI. Please bring any device paperwork, identification cards, or remotes with you.**

**For your safety, your scan cannot be performed until we are able to verify the device information. If we cannot confirm that your implant is MRI-safe, we will need to reschedule the exam until this information is available.**

##### **Who shouldn’t get an MRI?**

**MRI is a safe and non-invasive exam for most people, but it’s not right for everyone. Because MRI uses strong magnetic fields, certain metal objects or implanted devices can pose safety risks or interfere with image quality. You should not have an MRI (or should first speak with your provider or technologist) if you have:**

* **Certain implanted devices – such as pacemakers, cochlear implants, some aneurysm clips, or older neurostimulators that are not MRI-safe.**  
* **Metal fragments or shrapnel in the body – especially near the eyes or brain.**  
* **Recent surgery involving metal implants or stents that haven’t yet been verified as MRI-compatible.**  
* **External or internal medical devices like insulin pumps, medication patches with metallic backing, or external fixators.**  
* **Pregnancy in the first trimester, unless the MRI is medically necessary and approved by your physician.**

**Before your scan, our technologist will review a detailed safety questionnaire to make sure MRI is safe for you. Always tell your provider or imaging staff if you have any metal implants, devices, or medical conditions that may affect your exam.**

##### **What if I’m claustrophobic or anxious?**

**You’re not alone, many people feel nervous about being in the MRI machine. Please let us know ahead of time so we can make your experience as easy and comfortable as possible.**

## **CT**

##### **What is a CT scan?**

**A CT (computed tomography) scan uses X-rays and a computer to create detailed cross-sectional images of your body. It’s faster than most other imaging and is excellent for bones, lungs, the abdomen, and emergency evaluations.**

##### **What’s the difference between an MRI and a CT?**

**An MRI uses a powerful magnet and radio waves- no radiation- to capture very detailed images of soft tissues like the brain, nerves, muscles, tendons, and ligaments. A CT uses X-rays and is very fast, making it ideal for bones, lungs, the abdomen, and emergent scans. Your provider will choose the right test for your situation.**

##### **Do I need a doctor’s order for a CT?**

**Yes. CT scans use x-ray radiation, which means they require a provider’s before it can be performed. This ensures the test is medically appropriate and that the benefits outweigh any radiation exposure.**

##### **How much does a CT cost? Do you take insurance?**

**We accept most insurance plans and also offer transparent self-pay pricing. You’ll know costs up front, with no surprises.**

##### **Is CT safe? What about radiation?**

**CT uses a small amount of ionizing radiation. Our state-of-the-art scanners are equipped with dose-reduction technology and low dose protocols, and our expert technologists use ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) principles. This keeps exposure as low as possible while still getting clear images.**

##### **Do I need contrast? Is it safe?**

**Some CT exams use an iodine-based contrast dye to highlight organs and blood vessels. Most people tolerate it well. Tell us if you’ve had a prior contrast reaction, asthma, kidney disease, or are pregnant. If contrast is recommended by your provider, our expert technologist will go through a brief safety check and walk you through the contrast process step by step.**

##### **How should I prepare?**

**For most non-contrast CTs, no special prep is needed. If contrast is planned, you may be asked not to eat for 2–4 hours and to arrive a little early. Our staff will give you exact instructions at the time you schedule. Wear comfortable, metal-free clothing and bring a list of your medications.**

##### **What if I’m pregnant or think I might be?**

**Please tell us before your scan. Because CT uses X-rays, we do not perform them on women who are pregnant or think they could be. Be sure to discuss non radiation alternatives with your provider.**

##### **I’m claustrophobic – will a CT be hard for me?**

**CT scanners are short and open at both ends. Like a donut\! Most scans take only a few minutes, and you can see out the whole time. Let us know if you’re anxious; we’ll talk you through the exam and keep you comfortable.**

##### **How long does a CT take?**

**Most CT exams take 5–10 minutes. If your scan is with contrast, plan 30-45 minutes including setup.**

##### **When will I get results and who reads my scan?**

**A board-certified radiologist interprets your images and sends a report to your provider. Results are typically available within 24–48 hours (often sooner). Our staff can provide a QR code, a disc or even help you access your images and results with our [online patient portal](https://firstchoice-imaging.com/patients/).**
